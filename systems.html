A note on the obvious question

There are plenty of AI chief-of-staff tools now: meeting bots, inbox triage, daily briefings. I build with systems like these; I'm not one of them. Tools transcribe your decisions. A Chief of Staff makes them with you, owns the follow-through, and is accountable when it matters. The AI is my leverage. The judgment is the job.

The judgment

Reads what people actually need.

Not what they say. The gap between the two is where most expensive mistakes happen, and where I've made a habit of stepping in before they do.

01

A long-term US customer at CampK12 was unhappy, but the complaint didn't fully add up. Subtle Spanish phrasing in her calls pointed at the real ask: not a refund, a bilingual coding instructor for her child. Repositioned the request internally. Kept the account, avoided a refund and a possible public complaint.

02

Joined CampK12 in the early days of the pandemic, when loss and uncertainty were constant across the team. Started a simple appreciation habit in the company WhatsApp group. It held: zero attrition across three full years there, and former teammates have thanked me for it long after we all moved on.

03

A client's hiring mandate at Wroots Global called for a Senior Data Scientist, but the budget didn't match that title anywhere in the market. Went back and asked more questions instead of running a search set up to fail. The real need was narrower: data visualisation, Tableau, Power BI, SQL. Repositioned the mandate and delivered the hire.

04

Early in my career, a piece of assigned work fell outside my skill set. Rather than say no and let the workflow stall, I sourced it out through a freelance marketplace and kept delivery on track.
